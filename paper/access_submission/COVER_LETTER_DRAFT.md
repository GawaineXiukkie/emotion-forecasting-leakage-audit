Dear Editor-in-Chief,

Please consider our manuscript, “Information-Matched Auditing of Conversational
Emotion-Shift Forecasting,” for publication in IEEE Access as a Research Article.

Conversational emotion forecasting is intended to predict an emotional change before the
target utterance is observed. We show that common evaluation practices can silently violate
this temporal contract or reward degenerate predictors. The manuscript contributes a
target-absent evaluation protocol, executable causality and leakage audits, information-matched
train-fold-only transition baselines, equal validation search budgets for six causal model
families, seed--dialogue-aware inference, a same-architecture leakage dose response, and a
full distinction between interaction-level next-turn shift and same-person next-own-utterance
shift. The accompanying code, raw seed predictions, and reviewable artifacts support
independent reproduction.

The principal empirical finding is conditional on information availability. In the deployable
comparison, all six models improve in point estimate over a transition baseline driven by
train-only predicted current labels on all four primary text corpora; duplicate-free DailyDialog
gains of up to 0.167 ROC--AUC survive a joint 72-comparison Holm correction. When both sides
receive gold current emotion, the transition baseline remains competitive on IEMOCAP and stronger
on MELD, while all six DailyDialog gains remain corrected-significant. A speaker-relation audit
and full self-shift benchmark clarify what adjacent-turn change does and does not measure. We
believe these findings are relevant to IEEE Access readers working in affective computing, speech
and language processing, multimodal learning, and reliable machine-learning evaluation.
